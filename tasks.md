  Biggest opportunity: you are not charging what you're worth, and the offer structure
   isn't helping you fix that                                                         
                                                                                      
  1. Pricing — the most expensive gap                                                 
                                                                                      
  What's happening: £200/session for an ILM L5 coach with VP/CTO experience, 300+     
  people coached, 14 years engineering, and sector-specific expertise (neurodivergent,
   queer, tech). The 3-session bundle saves the buyer £100 (I recalculate:            
  3×£200=£600, bundle=£500, saving=£100 not £50 — but still only 17%). That's a       
  meaningful saving now, but the framing ("go deeper") undersells it as a commitment  
  device.

  Why it matters: Your credentials are in the top tier of coaching CVs specifically   
  for the tech sector. A standard exec coach with no tech background charges
  £200–£400/session. You have the domain expertise plus the lived experience plus the 
  ILM qualification. You are underpricing by at least £50–100/session, and possibly
  more. At £200 you are attracting self-funding ICs. At £300 you are still accessible
  and start attracting companies paying for their managers.

  What to do:                                                                         
  - Raise single session to £250 minimum, £300 is defensible on credentials alone.
  - Restructure the bundle: 3 sessions at £700 (was £600) with bundle price £550.     
  Saving becomes £150 — now it actually changes behaviour.                       
  - Add framing: "most coaches at this credential level charge £300–£500/session" is  
  not bragging, it's anchoring. You don't have to say it explicitly — the credentials
  strip can do it visually.                                                           
         
  ---                                                                                 
  2. Missing tier: there's nothing between £500 and "hire me as an interim CTO"
                                                                                      
  What's happening: The value ladder is: free blog → paid course (no price on this
  site, external) → 3 sessions £500 → nothing. There is no ongoing engagement, no     
  retainer, no "work with me for 3 months" offer. The 3-session bundle is a ceiling,
  not a gateway.                                                                      
         
  Why it matters: Some of your clients are already in a situation where 3 sessions    
  won't cut it — a new EM navigating a restructure, someone building toward a
  promotion over 6 months, a neurodivergent person rebuilding after burnout. Right now
   they buy the bundle and then have to actively re-purchase. You're leaving ongoing
  revenue on the table and creating unnecessary friction for the clients who need the
  most support.

  What to do:
  - Add a 6-session programme, £900–1000, badged as "for the messy stuff that doesn't
  resolve in three conversations." This is not a new product — it's just letting      
  people buy more at a commit-and-save rate.                                    
  - Consider a monthly retainer format (2 sessions/month + async check-in via         
  email/voice note) at £350–400/month. That's lower per-session but recurring. A
  handful of retainer clients dramatically stabilises income.                         
   
  ---                                                                                 
  3. The corporate buyer is invisible
                                                                                      
  What's happening: There is no indication on the site that organisations can buy
  coaching for their managers. The CTAs all point to individual email enquiries. The  
  credibility strip (300+ people, VP/CTO, $300M ARR) speaks directly to a corporate
  buyer — but there's nowhere for them to land.                                       
         
  Why it matters: Companies pay 2–3× what individuals pay, buy in blocks, and your EM 
  coaching is precisely what a startup CTO wants for their newly-promoted managers.
  "Holy Shit I'm a Manager™" is practically a corporate L&D product already. One team 
  buys 3 sessions each = more revenue than 6 individual clients.

  What to do: Add a single line to the coaching pages: "Working with a team? I coach  
  engineering managers in bulk for startups and scale-ups. [Get in touch]." That's it
  for now. You don't need a whole page — just a door.                                 
         
  ---
  4. The course is working as a dead end, not a funnel
                                                                                      
  What's happening: The course is linked from the EM coaching page in a "coaching or
  course?" split, which actively routes people away from the higher-value product. The
   framing positions them as alternatives, not a sequence.
                                                                                      
  Why it matters: "Course gives you the foundations, coaching handles the live mess"  
  is correct but the copy is doing it wrong. Someone arriving at that section is
  already warm on coaching — you then offer them a cheaper alternative and send them  
  to an external site. Some of them will take it and never come back.

  What to do:
  - Flip the framing: "Take the course first, then come back when it gets live." Make
  the course the entry point to coaching, not the budget option.                      
  - Add a sentence somewhere on the course (if you control any of that copy) pointing
  back to 1:1 coaching for live situations.                                           
  - On the coaching page, the course callout should come after pricing, not between   
  the session description and the testimonials. Right now it creates doubt at the
  wrong moment.                                                                       
         
  ---                                                                                 
  5. Testimonial gap: nothing from the neurodivergent/folks-in-tech audience
                                                                                      
  What's happening: All four testimonials on the site are about EM promotions and
  management coaching. The folks-in-tech page uses the same four. There is no         
  testimonial from someone who came to you as a neurodivergent person, a queer person,
   someone navigating burnout, someone fighting for accommodations, or someone who    
  just needed to stop masking at work.

  Why it matters: The folks-in-tech audience has to take it on faith that you actually
   serve them. The EM testimonials do speak to the quality of your work but they don't
   speak to that audience's specific concern: "will this person actually get it?" Your
   lived-experience positioning is strong in the copy but there's zero social proof
  for it.

  What to do: Ask two or three folks-in-tech clients for a testimonial. Brief them    
  specifically: what were you dealing with, what changed, what was it like to work
  with a coach who also has lived experience? One good one on the folks-in-tech page  
  would do more than all four EM testimonials combined for that audience.

  Why it matters: Founders are a high-willingness-to-pay audience with a specific,    
  acute problem (isolation, decision-making under pressure, people chaos with no HR
  support, often first-time managers). You have 150 of them on your record. That is a

  What to do:
  - At minimum: add "founders and early-stage CEOs" to the folks-in-tech page as an
  explicitly named audience.                                                          
  - Medium move: add a third coaching path. Not a full new page immediately, but a
  card on the coaching split page: "I'm a founder with no one to think out loud with."
   Link to the folks-in-tech page for now, but name them. Being named matters.
