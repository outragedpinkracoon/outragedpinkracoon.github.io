I've worked in tech for nearly two decades, from hands-on engineering all the way up to exec. I've seen some sh*t I tell you.

I'm Valerie Dryden — Fractional CTO / VP Engineering and Executive Coach. I work with founders, CTOs, engineering managers, and folks in tech on the messy, human, hard parts of working in tech.

I've been in every room this work takes you to and I've seen every brand of chaos there is. From tiny start up to Series G unicorns.

If you want corporate polish, I am probably not your person.

If you want autistic directness, kindness, hard-won judgement, and someone who has been in this exact mess — welcome.

I work in two modes

As an Executive Coach I use everything I've learned to coach people through the exact situations I've been in: the messy people issues, the difficult strategic decisions, the career crossroads, the overwhelm, the moments where you genuinely don't know what to do next and can't admit that to anyone.

As a Fractional CTO / VP Engineering I embed directly, on the ground, no corporate bullshit. Tech due diligence, tech and culture assessments, delivery transformation, AI adoption. I'll tell you what you need to hear and make a plan to get you where you need to be.

Pick your adventure.

I'm an engineering manager and I'm out of my depth

You got promoted. Congratulations. Also: holy shit. Feedback, one-to-ones, conflict, team chaos, managing up — and the deeply cursed reality that there is no undo button for people.

EM coaching →

I'm a founder or CTO making hard calls alone

No peer group, no safety net, and everyone around you needs you to look like you know what you're doing. I've spent six years coaching founders through exactly this.

Founder coaching →

I want more power, more credit, and less bullshit

You're good at this. That's not the problem. Career moves, burnout, masking, visibility, and deciding what the hell happens next — especially if you're neurodivergent, queer, or disabled.

Folks in tech coaching →

Something is broken in my engineering org and I need to know why

Delivery chaos, CTO gap, expensive decisions you can't afford to get wrong, a board asking hard questions, or a team that's outgrown its own operating system.
